The Era of Giant Worlds

In 1995, astronomers discovered 51 Pegasi b, the first confirmed exoplanet orbiting a sun-like star. This "hot Jupiter" was twice the size of our own Jupiter, orbiting incredibly close to its star. This discovery marked the beginning of a revolution in our understanding of planetary systems.

The Search for Smaller Worlds

As detection methods improved and missions like Kepler launched, astronomers began finding smaller and smaller planets. The average size of discovered exoplanets has decreased dramatically over time, revealing that Earth-sized worlds are actually quite common in our galaxy.

Distribution Shifts Over Time

This ridge plot shows how the distribution of planet sizes has evolved. Early discoveries were dominated by large gas giants, but recent years have revealed a wealth of smaller, rocky planets. The shift reflects both improved detection sensitivity and the true abundance of smaller worlds.

Different Methods, Different Worlds

Different detection methods reveal different types of planets. Transit photometry (like Kepler) excels at finding small, close-in planets. Radial velocity detects larger planets at various distances.
